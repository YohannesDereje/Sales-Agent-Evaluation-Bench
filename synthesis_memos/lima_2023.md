# Synthesis Memo: LIMA — Less Is More for Alignment
**Paper:** Zhou et al., NeurIPS 2023
**Author:** Yohannes | **Date:** 2026-05-01

---

## What the Paper Argues

Zhou et al. introduce the **Superficial Alignment Hypothesis**: a large pretrained model already contains the knowledge it needs to perform most tasks. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT) does not teach new knowledge — it teaches the model the *style and format* of compliant outputs. The evidence: 1,000 carefully curated training examples produce outputs that human raters prefer over models trained on 52,000 mediocre examples. Doubling data from 2,000 to 32,000 examples produced almost no quality gain when diversity was not added.

The operational recommendation is clear: **invest in quality filtering over raw volume**. A small, high-quality dataset with genuine diversity across tasks and formats outperforms a large, noisy dataset in the same domain.

---

## Where I Disagree — and Why My Evidence Supports It

**LIMA's 1,000-pair saturation finding was validated on a 65B model. At 2B scale, the pretrained knowledge base is narrower and 1,000 pairs is likely insufficient for reliable calibration.**

LIMA's central experiment used LLaMA-65B as the backbone. A 65B model has seen vastly more training data than a 2B model and has a correspondingly richer internal representation of professional writing conventions, domain vocabulary, and tone variation. When LIMA says "1,000 examples are enough to teach format," it is implicitly assuming a backbone that already has strong representations of the target behavior.

Qwen 3.5 2B is our backbone. At 2B parameters, the model has weaker representations of domain-specific professional register — the B2B technical staffing context, the Tenacious tone markers, the specific claim-proportionality constraint — than a 65B model would. The SOC-01 failure itself is evidence of this: the model defaults to confident sales language because its training data contains far more examples of assertive outreach than of hedged, signal-proportional outreach. A 65B model fine-tuned with 1,000 LIMA-style examples might correct this default; a 2B model may need closer to 3,000 examples to achieve the same calibration because it has a smaller prior to update.

My training target of **1,000–3,000 pairs** (not a fixed 1,000) reflects this scale adjustment. The upper bound of 3,000 is not arbitrary — it is where LIMA's own data suggests diminishing returns begin even at larger scales, and where our dataset generation budget runs out. If post-training evaluation shows the model is not calibrated at 1,000 pairs, we extend to 3,000 before concluding that training failed.

---

## What I Adopt From the Paper

- **Quality filtering over volume:** `sft_pairs_filtered.jsonl` includes only training pairs where the assistant output scores ≥0.70 on the scoring rubric. A lower-quality pair teaches the wrong behavior regardless of how many examples surround it.
- **Diversity across failure dimensions:** The 30/30/25/15 source-mode mix and the 10-dimension seed coverage ensure training data is not concentrated in SOC examples only — following LIMA's finding that diversity matters more than quantity.
- **The framing of the problem:** SOC-01 is a calibration gap (the model knows what a professional email looks like; it has not learned to *constrain claim strength to match signal confidence*). This framing — format, not knowledge — is what justifies SFT as the right intervention over retrieval augmentation or prompt engineering alone.
