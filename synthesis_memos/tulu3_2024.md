# Synthesis Memo: Tülu 3 — Pushing Frontiers in Open Language Model Post-Training
**Paper:** Ivison et al., 2024
**Author:** Yohannes | **Date:** 2026-05-01

---

## What the Paper Argues

Ivison et al. describe the data mixing and training pipeline behind Tülu 3, an open post-training framework that achieves competitive performance with proprietary models. Their central claim about SFT mixing: **effective mixing is iterative and benchmark-driven**. The team ran multiple training runs with different source-mode proportions, evaluated on held-out benchmarks after each run, and adjusted the mix based on which dimensions showed the most degradation. Key findings: small amounts of domain-specific data (30k–35k examples at their scale) produce large, targeted skill gains; diverse conversational data prevents degradation of general capabilities; domain-specific and general data are largely additive.

The operational recommendation: treat data mixing as a hyperparameter to be optimized over multiple training runs, using benchmark performance as the feedback signal.

---

## Where I Disagree — and Why My Evidence Supports It

**Iterative benchmark-driven mixing is a scale-dependent technique. At our dataset size (≤3,000 training pairs), the mixing ratio has no measurable impact and iterative ablation wastes the majority of our compute budget.**

Tülu 3's iterative mixing approach was validated at 939k prompts across dozens of training runs on A100 clusters. The technique works because at that scale, different source-mode proportions produce meaningfully different benchmark outcomes — the signal-to-noise ratio of a mixing ablation is high enough to guide decisions.

At 1,000–3,000 training pairs on a T4 GPU, this is not true. LIMA's saturation finding makes this concrete: performance saturates at ~1,000–3,000 pairs for models this size regardless of source distribution, because the total training signal is bounded by the dataset size, not by the mixing ratio. A 70/15/10/5 split and a 30/30/25/15 split of 2,000 pairs will produce similar results because both provide the model with enough examples of each failure dimension to update its prior — the marginal effect of the ratio is small relative to the noise in a single T4 training run.

More practically: our $10 budget allows at most 2–3 training runs total. Tülu 3's iterative approach requires 10+ runs to converge. Running iterative mixing ablations would consume our entire compute budget before we had results to report.

My design decision: use a **fixed 30/30/25/15 source-mode split** (trace_derived / programmatic / synthesis / hand_authored) chosen to ensure all 6 failure dimensions have representation in the training data, not to optimize benchmark performance. The split reflects coverage targets, not a mixing hyperparameter search. This is the right decision at our scale and budget — and LIMA's findings justify it.

---

## What I Adopt From the Paper

- **4-mode data mix philosophy:** Tülu 3's core insight — that diversity across source modes prevents capability degradation on dimensions not directly targeted by training — justifies maintaining all 4 source modes rather than collapsing to programmatic-only (which would give us SOC coverage but minimal signal on BOC, TD, MTL, ICP).
- **Held-out as unseen skill test:** Tülu 3's "unseen evaluation suite" strategy — evaluating on benchmarks the model was not trained toward — validates our sealed held-out partition. We evaluate on tasks the model has never seen to measure skill transfer, not memorization.
- **Domain-specific data is additive:** Tülu 3's finding that domain-specific data improves targeted skills without hurting general capabilities supports our decision to train only on Tenacious-domain examples. We do not need general conversational data in the mix to preserve the backbone's general writing ability.
